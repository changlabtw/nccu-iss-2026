In-Class Lab Guide

Digital Communication and Emerging Technologies

In-Class Lab Guide (Instructor Notes + Student Worksheets)

NCCU ISS 2026

How to use this guide

  • Each day runs 09:10–10:30 lecture, a 10-minute break, then the hands-on lab from about 10:45 to 12:00 (roughly 1.25 hours). The lab is fully browser-based, so it works on the library lab computers with no installation and no personal laptop required.

  • We standardize on Claude (claude.ai) as the course LLM, so it stays consistent with the AI-agent session and the NotebookLM handout.

  • Each student works on their own individual project (about 8 students, so about 8 projects). Seat students so they can still talk to a neighbour and compare results, even though the project is solo.

  • Days 1 to 5 are hands-on labs (below). Days 3 to 5 build one final project (a topic chatbot that answers from your own documents), which each student presents on Day 6.

Day 1 (7/20): AI Big Picture & Neural Network Intuition

Goal: Build intuition for how a neural network learns, with no coding.

Tools: TensorFlow Playground (playground.tensorflow.org); account setup for Google/Colab, Claude, and NotebookLM.

Run of show

  • 0:00–0:10 Account setup checklist (Google/Colab, Claude, NotebookLM); help your neighbor.

  • 0:10–0:15 Quick round of introductions; explain that each student will build their own project across Days 3 to 5.

  • 0:15–0:28 Guided tour of Playground (features, hidden layers, neurons, output, loss).

  • 0:28–1:00 Challenge worksheet (below).

  • 1:00–1:10 Share best networks; discuss overfitting.

  • 1:10–1:15 Wrap-up and link to Day 2.

Student worksheet

  • Open playground.tensorflow.org, choose the Circle dataset, and press play. Watch the loss go down.

  • Set hidden layers to 0. Can the model separate the data? Why or why not?

  • Add one hidden layer with 2 neurons and classify the Circle. Record your test loss.

  • Switch to the Spiral dataset. Challenge: reach test loss below 0.1 using as few neurons as possible. Record your architecture.

  • Make the network large (for example 6 neurons by 4 layers) and compare training loss with test loss. The gap is overfitting.

Deliverable: A screenshot of your best Spiral network, plus two sentences: what adding neurons or layers did, and what overfitting means in your own words.

Instructor notes

  • Runs in any browser on lab machines. Keep the math out; focus on intuition.

  • Early finishers: try changing the activation function or the learning rate and observe the effect.

  • Common confusion: features versus neurons. Clarify with a quick board sketch.

Day 2 (7/21): Train an Image Classifier (Fashion-MNIST)

Goal: Train a real image classifier and see how design choices change accuracy and overfitting.

Tools: Google Colab (pre-built notebook, mostly run-only with clearly marked edit cells); Fashion-MNIST dataset. Lab notebook: Fashion-MNIST Colab.

Run of show

  • 0:00–0:08 Open the shared Colab notebook; File > Save a copy in Drive.

  • 0:08–0:25 Run the load and view cells; look at the 10 clothing classes and sample images.

  • 0:25–0:50 Build and train the model; read training and validation accuracy; find a misclassified image.

  • 0:50–1:05 Parameter experiment: change one thing at a time and re-run.

  • 1:05–1:15 Compare results with your neighbours; discuss overfitting.

Student worksheet

  • Open the Colab link, then File > Save a copy in Drive so you can edit your own copy.

  • Run the first cells to load Fashion-MNIST. Look at the 10 classes (T-shirt, trouser, and so on) and view a few sample images.

  • Run the model-building and training cells. Record the final training accuracy and validation accuracy.

  • Run the show-predictions cell. Find one image the model got wrong and note what it guessed.

  • Parameter experiment. Change one item at a time, re-run training, and record the validation accuracy each time:

  • Increase epochs from 5 to 15. Better or worse? Any sign of overfitting?

  • Change the hidden layer size (for example 128 to 32, then to 256).

  • Add a second hidden layer.

  • Decide which setting gave the best validation accuracy and write it down.

Deliverable: Your best validation accuracy and the settings that produced it, plus one misclassified image (screenshot) with a one-line guess at why.

Instructor notes

  • Share the notebook link or a QR code in advance; mark editable cells clearly (for example, ### CHANGE ME ###).

  • Fashion-MNIST loads from keras.datasets and trains fast on CPU, so no GPU is needed. Keep a results-cached copy as a fallback if Colab is slow.

  • Advanced students can add a Dropout layer and observe how the train and validation gap changes.

Day 3 (7/22): Prompt Engineering + choose your project

Goal: Learn to steer a language model with prompt engineering, then pick your own final-project topic and draft its system prompt. Days 3 to 5 build one project: a topic chatbot that answers from documents you provide (a RAG app), presented on Day 6.

Tools: Google Colab; a free OpenRouter API key; free models (google/gemma-4-31b-it:free, openai/gpt-oss-20b:free). Example notebook: Prompt Engineering Colab. Free model IDs rotate; if a model returns a 404, pick another from openrouter.ai/models (filter: free). Homework template: template.

Run of show

  • 0:00–0:10 Get a free OpenRouter API key; open the example Colab; make your first model call.

  • 0:10–0:35 Five prompt techniques, one at a time: persona/role, format constraints, few-shot examples, precise description, and chain-of-thought. Change the system and user prompt, re-run, and note what improved.

  • 0:35–0:50 “Who’s on First” activity: run the same ambiguous dialogue through two or three different free models and compare how each one reasons.

  • 0:50–1:10 Choose your final-project topic (be creative; it can relate to your own field) and draft a system prompt that uses at least two techniques.

  • 1:10–1:15 Share topics; preview Day 4 (giving your chatbot real knowledge with RAG).

Student worksheet

  • In the example Colab, compare the model’s answer with no prompt engineering versus with prompt engineering. Apply at least two techniques.

  • Try the persona technique: set the system prompt to a role (for example, “You are a biologist who can answer any question about living things”) and see how the answers change.

  • Add a format constraint (for example, “list exactly five items, no introduction or conclusion”) and a one-shot example.

  • Write one sentence on which single change helped most, and which two techniques you will use in your project’s system prompt.

Deliverable: Your project topic and a first system prompt that uses at least two prompt-engineering techniques, plus a short note on the difference prompt engineering made. Remove your API key before sharing the Colab link.

Instructor notes

  • The course standardizes on Claude for discussion, but this lab uses OpenRouter free models so every student can call an API in Colab without cost. Students can share one key between two or three people if rate limits bite.

  • The five techniques and the “Who’s on First” example come from the TA hands-on materials; keep example tasks close to humanities and social-science interests.

  • End on topic selection: this is the seed of the whole three-day project. Nudge students toward topics with findable text sources (a Wikipedia subject, a body of documents, a domain they know).

Day 4 (7/23): Give your chatbot knowledge with RAG

Goal: Build a retrieval-augmented (RAG) system so your chatbot answers from documents you provide, not just its training data. (Today’s lecture, AI at the Frontier of Science, is separate; this lab is your project build time.)

Tools: Google Colab (SentenceTransformer + FAISS) + OpenRouter free models. Example notebook: RAG Colab. Homework template: template. Sample texts: folder. Free model IDs rotate; if a model returns a 404, run the notebook’s “list free models” cell and pick a live one.

Run of show

  • 0:00–0:08 Why RAG: a model’s knowledge has a cutoff and can hallucinate; RAG lets it retrieve from your documents. The retrieve → augment → generate flow, and embedding + indexing in plain terms.

  • 0:08–0:20 Copy the RAG Colab, add your API key, and Run all.

  • 0:20–0:35 Gather your project texts: download two or three Wikipedia pages (or your own documents) as PDF and upload them (aim for at least 800 characters of real content).

  • 0:35–1:00 Query your RAG: ask an in-text question, a follow-up, an out-of-text question (does it refuse or hallucinate?), and a memory question (“what did I ask earlier?”).

  • 1:00–1:10 Guardrail challenge: like the Taipei Metro AI customer-service system that answered off-topic questions, how would you stop your bot from answering things outside its documents? Write your fix in the notebook.

  • 1:10–1:15 Save your chat log; preview Day 5 (turning this into a shareable web app).

Student worksheet

  • Run the RAG notebook on your uploaded texts and ask: What are the key points of these documents? Then a related follow-up question.

  • Ask one question the documents do not cover, and note whether the bot refuses or makes something up.

  • Ask “what have I asked so far?” to confirm the conversation memory works.

  • In the notebook, write one possible way to keep the bot on-topic (the guardrail challenge).

Deliverable: A working RAG notebook on your project topic, your uploaded texts, and a saved conversation log showing in-text, out-of-text, and memory questions. Remove your API key before sharing.

Instructor notes

  • Keep the embedding/indexing explanation conceptual (vectors as meaning coordinates; the index as a library catalog). The notebook hides the code; students mainly upload texts and ask questions.

  • The Wikipedia-PDF approach and the Taipei Metro guardrail case come from the TA RAG materials. The guardrail step is where the course’s trust and misinformation theme now lives.

  • This is dedicated project build time; circulate and help students whose retrieval returns weak answers (usually too little text, or texts with no relation to the questions).

Day 5 (7/27): Build and ship your AI app (Gradio)

Goal: Wrap your RAG system in a shareable web chat interface with Gradio, then assemble the final project (app + poster) for Day 6.

Tools: Google Colab + Gradio (gr.ChatInterface) + OpenRouter free models. Multi-file example / homework template: Gradio Colab. Final-project starter: project Colab.

Run of show

  • 0:00–0:10 Gradio in brief: gr.ChatInterface takes a function of (message, history) and returns a reply; launching gives you a public .gradio.live link anyone can open.

  • 0:10–0:30 Example 1: set your topic and persona, launch the chat UI, and open your public link on your phone.

  • 0:30–0:55 Example 2 (multi-file RAG): upload your project texts, load them, and ask cross-document questions (“what do these have in common?”).

  • 0:55–1:10 Assemble the final project: topic, texts, system prompt, and the running Gradio app. Start your poster.

  • 1:10–1:15 Save your app link and chat log; confirm Day 6 presentation order.

Student worksheet

  • In the multi-file example, upload at least two related documents and ask, in order: which files did I upload? What is each about? Do they share a common theme?

  • Give your chat interface a clear title and description for your topic.

  • Note your live .gradio.live link (it is temporary; keep a screenshot too) and download the chat log.

  • On your poster, state your app’s purpose, its likely users, and one limitation or guardrail.

Deliverable: A live Gradio RAG chatbot on your topic (shareable link plus screenshot), the conversation log, and a poster draft. This is your Day 6 final project.

Instructor notes

  • Gradio share links expire after a short time and the Colab session must stay open, so have students keep screenshots and be ready to relaunch for their presentation.

  • Free-model availability shifts: google/gemma-4-31b-it:free and openai/gpt-oss-20b:free are reliable defaults; check OpenRouter before class in case one changes.

  • The final-project brief (choose a topic, find texts, design a system prompt, build the app, make a poster) comes from the TA final-project materials. The poster and app are what each student presents on Day 6.

Day 6 (7/28): Final Presentations

Each student presents their individual final project: the topic chatbot (a Gradio RAG app) built across Days 3 to 5, with a poster covering the topic, the source texts, the system prompt, and one limitation or guardrail. Plan on about 7 minutes of talk plus 3 minutes of Q&A per student (10 minutes total); a live demo of the app is encouraged.

Run of show

  • 0:00–0:08 Set up; confirm presentation order; remind students of the rubric.

  • 0:08–0:48 Presentations 1–4, 10 minutes each (7 minutes talk, 3 minutes Q&A).

  • 0:48–0:58 Break.

  • 0:58–1:38 Presentations 5–8, same format.

  • 1:38–1:50 Peer feedback forms and short instructor comments.

  • 1:50–2:00 Course wrap-up and final reflection.

Slide outline (1-page template)

  • Title and your name.

  • The AI application or issue you chose.

  • How it works, in plain language.

  • Why it matters: its value or benefit.

  • Risks and ethics: misinformation, bias, trust, or privacy.

  • Connection to course concepts and your own hands-on results.

  • Conclusion and questions for the audience.

Grading rubric (share with students)

  • Content and accuracy.

  • Critical analysis: value plus a societal or ethical implication.

  • Connection to course concepts and hands-on labs.

  • Use and citation of your source texts (the documents behind your RAG app).

  • Delivery and clarity.

  • Response to questions.

  • Preparation: evidence you tested your app and can discuss its limits.

Peer feedback form (one per presenter, per audience member)

  • Two things this presenter did well.

  • One question you still have.

  • One concrete suggestion.
